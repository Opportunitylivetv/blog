Geometric Algebra is fascinating, and I believe solves a large number of problems that arise from a more traditional approach to vectors, but I've been very disappointed with the quality of books and explainations I've found, most of them zooming off into abstract realms too quickly, or spending an inordinate amount of time building up a generalized theory before finally getting to something useful.

Below is an explaination of Geometric Algebra that will start with a simple two dimensional vector space, i.e.:

\mathbb{R}^{2}

I'll presume a famialarity with Linear Algebra, and then we'll introduce the geometric product on that and we'll have the Geometric Alegebra over two dimensions:

\mathbb{G}^{2}

Our 2-D vector space has an orthonormal basis:

\bm{e_{1}} \perp \bm{e_{2}}

For example::

\bm{e_{1}} := (1,0)
\bm{e_{2}} := (0,1)

We know how to do vector addition and scalar multiplication of vectors, and that any vector can be represented as a linear combination of basis elements.

\begin{align*} \bm{a} &= -4 \bm{e_{1}} + 2 \bm{e_{2}} \\ \bm{b} &= 2 \bm{e_{1}} + 2 \bm{e_{2}} \\ \bm{a} + \bm{b} &= -2 \bm{e_1} + 4 \bm{e_2} \end{align*}

Linear Algebra doesn't define how to multiply two vectors, but Geometric Algebra does. We'll first start with the definition and then dig into what it means. The geometric product of the two vectors 'a' and 'b' is defined as:

\bm{a} \bm{b} := \bm{a} \cdot \bm{b} +\bm{a} \wedge \bm{b}

The first half of the equation is just the, inner product, or dot product from Linear Algebra, which can be found using either of the following definitions:

\bm{a} \cdot \bm{b} := ||\bm{a}|| ||\bm{b}|| \cos \theta
\bm{a} \cdot \bm{b} := \sum_{i}\bm{a_i}\bm{b_i}

Where θ is that angle between the two vectors.

Things to remember about the inner product are that it is 0 for orthogonal vectors:

\bm{e_{1}} \perp \bm{e_{2}} \implies \bm{e_1} \cdot \bm{e_2} = 0

And that a vector dot with itself gives the square of the norm of the vector, since \cos 0 = 1:

\bm{a} \cdot \bm{a} = {||\bm{a}||}^2

The inner product has the following properties, where α and β are scalar values.

\bm{a} \cdot \bm{b} = \bm{b} \cdot \bm{a} Commutative
\bm{a} \dcot (\bm{b} + \bm{c}) = \bm{a} \cdot \bm{b} + \bm{a} \cdot \bm{c} Distributive
(\alpha \bm{a})(\beta \bm{b}) = \alpha \beta (\bm{a} \cdot \bm{b}) Scalar Multiplication

The second part is called the wedge product, or exterior product, and given two vectors it is written as:

\bm{a} \wedge \bm{b}

The exterior product represents the oriented area defined by the two vectors, or more precisely is represents an oriented area in the plane defined by those vectors, also known as a bivector. There are two important aspects of this, the first is that the exact shape doesn't matter. For example, the bivectors represented below are equal because they have the same orientation (counter-clockwise) and the same area (3).

(1, 0) \wedge (0, 3) = (3, 0) \wedge (0, 1)

The second important factor is that the exterior product is anticommutative, that is, if you reverse the order of the vectors involved then the sign of the exterior product changes.

\bm{a} \wedge \bm{b} = - \bm{b} \wedge \bm{a}

Using two of the vectors above, note that the order that they are used in the exterior product will make the bivectors either clockwise or counter-clockwise.

The exterior product represents the oriented area defined by two vectors. In simple linear algebraic terms the parallelogram defined by two vectors:

\bm{a} = a_1 \bm{e_{1}} + a_2 \bm{e_{2}}
\bm{b} = b_1 \bm{e_{1}} + b_2 \bm{e_{2}}

Then the area of parallelogram defined by those two vectors is the absolute value of the determinant of:

\lvert det( \[ \begin{bmatrix} a_1 & a_2 \\ b_1 & b_2 \end{bmatrix} \] ) \rvert
